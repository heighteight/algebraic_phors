




Reviewer A:

> Does your semantics make it possible to observe similar phenomena for PHORS? 

This is a very interesting question, but we are not sure that the results introduced in this paper alone are enough to answer it.

> As mentioned above, I would appreciate further intuition about the fragment PBHORS^infty. 

From a syntactic viewpoint, PBHORS^infty adds some subject expansion to the finitary system.
If a PBHORS^{<infty} contains an equation of the form Fx=t, where t includes the nonterminal G, 
we can construct a PHORS with the same termination behaviour by changing the equation in  Fg​x=t[G∖g]
and replacing every occurrence of F by FG. The result is not a PBHORS^{<infty}, but it is indeed
a PBHORS^infty.
	
Reviewer B:

> There is insufficient discussion of prior work on the relationship between linear-logic semantics and formal power series.

We thank the reviewers for pointing out further references. The paper [ErhhardPT18] is our [16], cited in Section 4, and, while it deals with PCOH, it is certainly one of the main inspirations of our approach. 

> I wonder how to prove that this fixed point is Conway.

The proof is in Charles Grellois and Paul-André Melliès, An infinitary model of linear logic, property 3.

> What do you mean by the point-wise partial order? 

We indeed consider the pointwise order over the coefficients of the power series. 
In Theorem 6.7, that the minimal solution of the system is indeed a_G(1) can be shown using the fact that fixpoints in QRel are defined by iteration: given a polynomial equation x=p(x,z), its lfp a(z) is the sup of a^0(z)=0 and a^{n+1}(z)=p(a^n(z),z), while
the lfp y of x=p(x,1) is the sup of y^0=0, y^{n+1}=p(y^n,1); as evaluation in 1 is a Scott continous semiring homomorphism, we can prove by induction over n that a^{n}(1)=y^n and then a(1)=y.

> Is there anything that I missed in Section 7?

It is true that our discussion focuses a lot on the syntactic translation, but the core of the argument is given by Lemma 7.3 which shows that, under suitable conditions, the fixpoint of a PHORS^infty coincides with a fixpoint computed with parameters from P (in the statement of the lemma we should have highlighted more that the final fixpoint in RRel(P, N) can be seen as a fixpoint in R{{P}}Rel(0,N)). The translation provides a syntactic way to recover this underlying parametric fixpoint. Concerning the examples, the parameters disappear in the case of a closed PHORS, as they are all replaced by non terminals, but would remain for a non-closed one (for example, if we allow other constants than e )

> Is it possible to decide AST solely by the algebraicity? 

It is worth to stress that our results rely on a definition of "algebraic fps" as minimal solution of a system of (polynomial) equations. This is an adaptation of the traditional field-theoretic notion to semirings and is well-established in combinatorics [6] and formal language theory [33] (but also Droste and Kuich, Handbook of Finite Automata). It's the notion underlying Chomsky-Schützenberger's characterization of context-free languages via algebraic fps. Corollary 6.5.1—which states that these series are also algebraic in the traditional sense—is an auxiliary result that allows us to exploit well-known results from elimination theory. Still, this corollary could indeed be significant to obtain further decidability results (for instance, determining whether two PHORS share the same generating function). 

> The threshold problem for the second moment might also be decidable. 

Thanks for this remark! Indeed, the moments can be obtained by differentiating the power series, all of which can be expressed in the theory of the reals (while it is not clear to us how this could be done following the approach of [37]). Nevertheless, as is for AST and PAST, we think that the fixed-point notion of algebraicity remains essential. 


\Reviewer C

> How can you be sure that these sets have solutions in [0,1]? 

This follows from the adequacy of the weighted relational model [8] (our Proposition 4.1): this indeed ensures that the coefficients of a_G(z) coincide with the probability of terminating with a certain number of choices, so all such coefficients are in [0,1] and, moreover, their sum a_G(1) is also in [0,1] (as it is the global probability of termination).
