Points raised by the reviewers:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Review A:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \red{Does your semantics make it possible to observe similar phenomena for PHORS? }
		
		
		As we observe in Section 2, our semantics, based on power series with commuting variables, is not well-adapted to capture properties of trees: it essentially collapses different trees as soon as they have, for all $n$, the same number of branches of length $n$. For example, at ll. 274-279 we present a regular and a non context-free language having the same generating function. This simplification is useful in this context, as we are interested in the probability of termination, which only depends on the number of choices and not on their order. In particular, the system of algebraic equations arising from a $\mathrm{PBHORS}^{<\infty}$ o $\mathrm{PBHORS}^{\infty}$ of any order is always equivalent to the system arising from some order 1 PHORS, that, while possibly having a completely different tree, has the same termination probability. Because of this, it seems difficult to expect that this approach could reproduce the collapse at order 4, which appears to generate from finer (and not just probabilistic-theoretic) properties of PAHORS. In this regard, it could be interesting to study the case in which variables do not commute, that would produce a stricter correspondence between generating functions and languages.
	\end{itemize}

\item Review B:
	\begin{itemize}
	
	\item \red{I wonder how to prove that this fixed point is Conway.}
	
The proof is in [24], property 3.
	
	\item \red{What do you mean by the point-wise partial order?}
	
	We indeed consider the pointwise order over the coefficients of the power series. 
	In Theorem 6.7, that the minimal solution of the system is indeed $a_G(1)$ can be shown using the fact that fixpoints in QRel are defined by iteration: given a system of polynomial equations $(x_i=p(\vec x,z))_{i \in I}$, its lfp $a_I(z)$ is the sup of $a_I^0(z)=0$ and $a_I^{n+1}(z)=p(a_I^n(z),z)$, while
	the lfp y of $(x_i=p(\vec x,z))_{i \in I}$ is the sup of $y_I^0=0, y_I^{n+1}=p_I(y^n,1)$; as evaluation in 1 is a Scott continous semiring homomorphism, we can prove by induction over $n$ that $a_I^{n}(1)=y_I^n$, from which it follows that $a_I(1)=y$.
	
	\item\red{p.10, Remark 6.3: I believe that the coefficients of $a_G$ themselves can be computed directly and fairly easily}
	
	This is true, but it should noted that the formulas in [6] can be used to make asymptotic estimates that are not possible by a direct computation. In particular, according to Theorem 3 in [6], one could obtain estimates of the form $\mathbb{P}(\mathcal{G}\downarrow_n) \sim n^\alpha \rho^n $ for some $\alpha, \rho \in \mathbb{R}$. While the computation of these constants is not treated in this paper, the approach through algebraic fixpoint system opens the way to this kind of analysis, which would not be possible via direct computations.
\end{itemize}
